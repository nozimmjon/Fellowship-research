## Literature review

### Comparative benchmarks

The paper sits in the literature on intergenerational educational mobility, which studies how strongly parents' schooling predicts the schooling eventually attained by adult children. This literature matters for both equity and efficiency: schooling is a major channel through which advantage is transmitted across generations, and it is often measured more comparably than income or wealth in lower- and middle-income settings. Comparative evidence shows that educational persistence remains substantial and varies widely across countries rather than falling uniformly over time. Hertz et al. [@hertz_2008] document large cross-country differences in parent-child schooling association, and Causa and Johansson [@causa_johansson_2010] reach a similar conclusion for OECD settings. The GDIM database assembled by van der Weide et al. [@weide_etal_2024] extends that benchmark to 153 economies and reinforces the value of country-specific evidence where national mobility studies remain thin.

### Measure choice and design discipline

The literature also cautions against relying on a single mobility statistic. Torche [@torche_2015] emphasizes that intergenerational mobility measures are useful descriptors of opportunity structures but should not be treated as complete measures of equality of opportunity. Stromberg and Engzell [@stromberg_engzell_2025] show that country rankings in educational mobility can move with coding, ranking, and modeling choices. That methodological point maps directly onto this paper's design. A small locked bundle of measures, centered on a rank-rank slope, directional mobility rates, and same-category persistence, is more credible than a one-metric story, especially when questionnaire wording around parental schooling differs somewhat across LiTS waves.

### Transition settings and the Uzbekistan case

Evidence from transition economies suggests no single post-socialist mobility trajectory. Verashchagina [@verashchagina_2009] shows substantial cross-country variation in educational inheritance across post-communist settings, and Bruck and Esenaliev [@bruck_esenaliev_2018] find that Kyrgyzstan maintained relatively high educational mobility through transition. Turkey offers a complementary middle-income comparator: Aydemir and Yazici [@aydemir_yazici_2019] show strong regional and family-background gradients in educational mobility, while Akarcay-Gurbuz and Polat [@akarcay_polat_2017] illustrate what a stronger causal design requires by using exogenous provincial variation in schooling opportunities. Together, this literature supports treating Uzbekistan as an under-documented transition case rather than as a setting with a mechanically predictable mobility path.

### Uzbekistan's education context

Country diagnostics explain why the question matters in Uzbekistan. World Bank [@wb_uzb_2018] and UNICEF [@unicef_uzb_2021] document severe tertiary access constraints before the recent expansion of higher education, while UNICEF [@unicef_sitan_2023] and World Bank [@wb_gender_2024] highlight persistent rural-urban, migration-related, and gendered inequalities in the conditions that shape educational opportunity. These reports do not estimate intergenerational mobility directly. Their value for this paper is to show why parental background, place, and household support are plausible correlates of educational attainment in the Uzbek context.

### Pandemic background and the bounded role of Module C

The pandemic literature is relevant only as background for the LiTS IV child-module extension. UNICEF [@unicef_remote_2020] and Munoz-Najar et al. [@munoznajar_2021] emphasize that remote learning increased the salience of household support, devices, and connectivity. Survey evidence from Latvia and Poland likewise shows that parents absorbed a large share of the home-learning burden during school closures [@daniela_2021; @knopik_2021]. For Uzbekistan, Meliboeva, Patrinos, and Teixeira [@wb_blog_2020] document the early policy response, while Iqbal, Patrinos, and coauthors [@iqbal_patrinos_2025] show that pandemic-era learning outcomes do not reduce to a simple average-loss narrative. This literature justifies a short descriptive extension on disruption and support patterns, not a co-equal pandemic paper.

### Positioning the paper

The literature therefore supports a narrow contribution. The paper's most defensible contribution is a transparent multi-wave profile of educational persistence in Uzbekistan using repeated LiTS cross-sections, multiple complementary measures, and explicit limits on interpretation. Modules A and B belong in the descriptive and associational mobility literature; Module C is best read as a bounded, suggestive extension on household conditions during school disruption.
