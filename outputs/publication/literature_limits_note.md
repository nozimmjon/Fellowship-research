## Literature-based scope and identification limits

The literature supports a cautious interpretation strategy. Mobility statistics are informative summaries of intergenerational association, but they are not causal estimates of equality of opportunity [@torche_2015]. Measurement studies also show that mobility rankings can shift with coding and estimator choices [@stromberg_engzell_2025], which is why this paper reports a locked bundle of complementary metrics rather than a single headline statistic.

The core evidence uses repeated LiTS cross-sections, not a panel following fixed families. The design can therefore characterize population-level persistence patterns by wave, but it cannot isolate within-family causal change over time. This distinction is central for trend claims: descriptive persistence measures rise across waves, but pooled models do not estimate the change precisely once controls and fixed effects are added.

The causal literature shows what stronger identification would require. For example, Akarcay-Gurbuz and Polat [@akarcay_polat_2017] exploit exogenous provincial variation in schooling opportunity. The current Uzbekistan design does not include comparable exogenous region-time variation, so Modules A and B are appropriately interpreted as descriptive and associational.

The same boundary applies to the pandemic extension. Remote-learning studies motivate attention to devices, internet quality, and household support [@unicef_remote_2020; @munoznajar_2021], but the LiTS IV child module remains a small cross-sectional subsample. Module C can flag household vulnerability patterns, especially around education stoppage, but it cannot identify the causal effect of parental education net of all correlated constraints.

These limits are substantive design choices, not weaknesses to conceal. They keep the paper's claims aligned with what the available data can credibly support.
